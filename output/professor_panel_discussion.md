## Professor 7: Clinical Trialist (Phase III RCTs, failed AD therapeutics)

### Proposed Framing
Frame as a diagnostic taxonomy for WHY clinical trials fail at the mechanism level, validated against the two most expensive therapeutic research programs in history (AD and MS). The pitch to trialists: current post-hoc analysis of failed trials lumps "wrong target" with "wrong dose" with "wrong population" -- this framework provides a formal, testable classification that separates mechanism failure from design failure, demonstrated on 23 therapeutic-stratum claims including 6 independently null AD RCTs. The geometric mathematics should be presented as engineering scaffolding that produces the classification, not as the intellectual contribution itself.

### Proposed Title
> Classifying Mechanism Failure in Clinical Trials: A Formal Framework Validated on Alzheimer's and Multiple Sclerosis Therapeutics

### Main Contribution
A transport-obstruction taxonomy that formally distinguishes "right mechanism, wrong trial design" (evidence misfire) from "wrong mechanism entirely" (mimic mechanism) and from "mechanism varies across populations" (reference debt), validated by showing tiers predict therapeutic effect magnitude with tau=0.62 (p=0.0002) across 23 interventional claims.

### Target Journal
Clinical Trials (SAGE) -- the journal exists precisely for methodology that improves how we design, analyze, and interpret trials. The transport-obstruction taxonomy is a methods contribution about trial failure classification, not an epidemiology finding. Alternatively, Annals of Internal Medicine (Methods section) if the authors want broader clinical impact, but Clinical Trials reviewers will engage more seriously with the formalism and are less likely to reject on "not clinical" grounds.

### Strongest Result
The Fingolimod ARR-vs-CDP dissociation classified as MIMIC MECHANISM. This is the result that would make a trialist sit up, because it captures a phenomenon we all recognize but have no formal language for: a drug that hits its pharmacological target, reduces a surrogate endpoint (relapse rate), yet fails on the disability endpoint that actually matters. The framework gives this a name, a formal definition (the section over the efficacy claim rotates under transport from surrogate to clinical endpoint), and a testable prediction (mimic mechanisms will show high target engagement with null clinical benefit). Anti-tau CSF is the same pattern. These are not post-hoc labels -- they are derived from the obstruction classification and they match exactly what happened in the trials.

### Fatal Weakness
The Frechet variance "REALISM" result for 6 null AD RCTs (T=0.004) is presented as evidence that mechanism failure converges, but this conflates radically different failure modes. Bapineuzumab failed partly from ARIA toxicity and enrollment of APOE4 carriers at wrong doses. Solanezumab targeted soluble amyloid too late in disease course. Semagacestat (a gamma-secretase inhibitor) actually WORSENED cognition -- that is not a "null" result, it is a harmful one that implicates off-target Notch inhibition. Avagacestat had similar problems. These drugs converge to HR approximately 1.0 only if you squint at the primary endpoint and ignore the heterogeneity in WHY they failed. A trialist would say: six drugs failing for six different reasons is NOT convergent evidence that the amyloid mechanism is wrong -- especially now that lecanemab and donanemab show modest but real amyloid-clearing benefit. The framework must either (a) sub-classify these 6 nulls by failure mode and show the convergence holds within failure-mode strata, or (b) acknowledge that Frechet convergence of null results has a fundamentally different epistemology than convergence of positive results, or (c) risk looking naive to any reviewer who has run an AD trial.

### Must Add
A systematic failure-mode decomposition table for each of the 6 null AD RCTs, classifying each along the framework's own taxonomy: Was it evidence misfire (right target, wrong trial design -- dose, population, timing, endpoint), mimic mechanism (target engagement without clinical benefit, like anti-tau), or genuine mechanism disconfirmation? Then re-run the Frechet variance test within each failure-mode stratum. This is essential because the recent lecanemab/donanemab results demonstrate that amyloid WAS a valid mechanism all along for a subset of patients -- the earlier trials were evidence misfires, not mechanism failures. If the framework cannot retrospectively distinguish these cases, it fails its own stated purpose. Additionally, the WHIMS HRT finding must be explicitly classified: is it a therapeutic claim (HRT prevents dementia) that was disconfirmed, or a safety finding (HRT increases dementia risk in older women) that was confirmed? These are opposite conclusions from the same data, and the framework's handling of this determines whether it can manage the directionality of clinical claims.

### Must Cut
The fiber bundle and Grassmannian formalism in the main text. For a clinical trials audience, presenting the transport obstruction taxonomy in the language of differential geometry will cause immediate rejection -- not because the math is wrong, but because it creates the impression that the authors are importing unnecessary abstraction to dress up a classification scheme. Move the full geometric definitions to a mathematical appendix. In the main text, present the obstruction types operationally: "evidence misfire" means the claim changes tier when you control for trial design features; "mimic mechanism" means target engagement and clinical benefit decouple; "reference debt" means the effect estimate is not transportable across populations. These are testable, concrete definitions that trialists can evaluate without learning fiber bundles. The math can justify WHY these are the right categories, but it should not be the presentation layer.

### Key References
1. Cummings et al. (2022, 2024) "Alzheimer's disease drug development pipeline" -- the definitive serial review cataloging why AD trials fail, updated annually, which this framework's failure-mode taxonomy must engage with directly and show it adds value beyond Cummings' existing classification. 2. Salloway et al. (2014) "Two Phase 3 Trials of Bapineuzumab" and van Dyck et al. (2023) "Lecanemab in Early Alzheimer's Disease" -- these two papers bracket the amyloid hypothesis arc from failure to partial vindication, and the framework MUST demonstrate it can handle the fact that the same mechanism (amyloid clearance) was classified as failed in 2014 and validated in 2023 without the framework itself changing. 3. Fleming & DeMets (1996) "Surrogate end points in clinical trials: are we being misled?" -- the foundational paper on surrogate endpoint validity, which directly anticipates the "mimic mechanism" failure mode (surrogate responds, clinical endpoint does not) and provides the statistical framework that trialists already use for this problem.

## Professor 4: Neuroepidemiology Domain Expert (MS and AD cohort studies)

### Proposed Framing
Frame as a formal operationalization of evidence triangulation that reveals quantitative structure invisible to GRADE or Bradford Hill: specifically, the split calibration finding that etiologic and therapeutic evidence obey the same ordinal tier ranking but occupy statistically separable effect-magnitude regimes. The framework's contribution is not discovering the vitamin D paradox or the amyloid hypothesis failures (every neuroepidemiologist knows these) but providing a single scoring system whose tier-by-stratum interaction term (beta = -0.51, p = 0.032) quantifies the etiology-treatment translation gap that the field has discussed only qualitatively. Lead with the split calibration as the central empirical result, use the transport obstruction taxonomy as the mechanistic complement, and relegate the fiber bundle formalism to an appendix or supplement.

### Proposed Title
> Quantifying the Etiology-Treatment Translation Gap in Neuroepidemiology: A Tiered Validity Framework with Split Calibration

### Main Contribution
A five-tier validity scoring system, calibrated on 59 neuroepidemiological estimates across MS and AD, reveals that etiologic and therapeutic evidence maintain the same ordinal tier ranking (tau = 0.54) but occupy statistically separable effect-magnitude regimes (interaction p = 0.032), providing the first quantitative measure of the etiology-treatment translation gap.

### Target Journal
International Journal of Epidemiology (IJE). IJE published the foundational triangulation papers (Lawlor et al. 2016, Munafo and Davey Smith 2018) and routinely publishes methodological innovations in causal inference. The split calibration result directly extends the triangulation program by formalizing what triangulation detects qualitatively. The readership includes both methods-oriented epidemiologists and disease-specific researchers who would engage substantively with the MS/AD case studies. European Journal of Epidemiology is a reasonable alternative but has a smaller methodological audience.

### Strongest Result
The split calibration finding with the significant tier-by-stratum interaction (beta = -0.51, p = 0.032, overall R-squared = 0.38 unweighted, 0.68 weighted). This is the result that would genuinely surprise me as a neuroepidemiologist, because while we have long known informally that strong etiologic associations do not predict therapeutic success, nobody has quantified the magnitude of this gap across a structured evidence base with a formal tier system. The jackknife stability (range 0.524-0.575, all leave-one-out p-values below 0.05) addresses the obvious small-sample concern. If this interaction replicates across other disease pairs, it would have real implications for how we design translational research programs.

### Fatal Weakness
Circularity in tier assignment. The tiers encode study design quality (RCTs above MR above observational), and effect size magnitude correlates with study design for well-understood reasons (confounding inflates observational estimates, RCTs attenuate toward null). Unless the authors demonstrate that their tier assignments contain information BEYOND what a simple study-design hierarchy or GRADE rating already captures, the tau = 0.54 correlation may be an elaborate rediscovery of the fact that better-controlled studies yield smaller effect sizes. The paper must include a head-to-head comparison: assign each row a GRADE certainty rating, compute tau with GRADE, and show that MV tiers provide incremental predictive value. Without this, any epidemiology reviewer will reject on circularity grounds. Additionally, the tier assignments must be shown to have been made blind to effect size magnitude, or the entire calibration is contaminated.

### Must Add
A formal comparison against GRADE certainty ratings and a triangulation-based scoring on the same 59 rows. Compute Kendall tau for GRADE versus effect magnitude, then show that MV tiers add incremental predictive power in a partial-correlation or nested model comparison (likelihood ratio test). This is non-negotiable for an epidemiology audience. Also add a blinding protocol description: state explicitly whether tier assignments were made before or after effect sizes were extracted, ideally by independent raters with inter-rater reliability (Cohen kappa). If blinding was not done, acknowledge this as a limitation and propose a prospective validation design where tiers are assigned to ongoing trials before results are unblinded.

### Must Cut
The fiber bundle formalism (sections, connections, Grassmannians, cocycles) must be moved entirely to a mathematical appendix or supplement. IJE readers will not engage with it in the main text, and its presence in the body will cause desk rejection. The main text should describe the tier system, the Frechet variance test, and the transport obstruction taxonomy in plain epidemiological language, with a single paragraph noting that the formal mathematical foundations are developed in the supplement. The word "Grassmannian" should not appear in the abstract or introduction. Similarly, de-emphasize the AI interpretability origin story: epidemiologists will evaluate the framework on its epidemiological merits, and foregrounding the neural network connection risks the paper being dismissed as a methods tourism exercise rather than a substantive contribution to causal inference methodology.

### Key References
1. Lawlor DA, Tilling K, Davey Smith G (2016). Triangulation in aetiological epidemiology. International Journal of Epidemiology 45(6):1866-1886 -- THE foundational paper on using convergence across study designs with different bias structures to strengthen causal inference; the Frechet variance test is essentially a formalization of triangulation and must be positioned relative to it. 2. Munafo MR, Davey Smith G (2018). Robust research needs many lines of evidence. Nature 553:399-401 -- the widely-cited manifesto arguing that replication alone is insufficient and that independent evidence streams with orthogonal biases are required; directly relevant to the invariance depth metric. 3. Bjornevik K et al. (2022). Longitudinal analysis reveals high prevalence of Epstein-Barr virus associated with multiple sclerosis. Science 375(6578):296-301 -- the landmark study providing the HR=32.4 estimate that anchors the etiologic stratum; must be cited with appropriate context about the 20-year military cohort design and the near-universal EBV seroconversion that makes the denominator extremely small.

## Professor 8: Mendelian Randomization Methodologist

### Proposed Framing
Frame the paper as a formal framework for evidence triangulation that exposes precisely WHERE and WHY different causal inference designs (observational, MR, RCT) disagree, replacing the informal "triangulation" heuristic with geometric structure. The transport obstruction classification is the hook for MR methodologists: it forces authors to specify which MR assumption fails when MR and observational evidence diverge, rather than defaulting to "confounding unmasked." Position the Frechet variance test as a quantitative successor to Lawlor et al.'s qualitative triangulation framework, one that can formally distinguish "convergent evidence" from "concordant bias."

### Proposed Title
> Transport Obstructions in Causal Evidence: A Geometric Framework for Diagnosing Discordance Between Mendelian Randomization, Observational, and Experimental Estimates

### Main Contribution
A formal geometric framework that classifies WHY different causal inference designs (MR, observational, RCT) disagree on the same exposure-outcome pair, moving beyond the informal triangulation heuristic to a testable obstruction taxonomy with quantitative convergence statistics.

### Target Journal
International Journal of Epidemiology (IJE) -- this is where Lawlor et al. (2016) published the triangulation framework, where major MR methods papers appear (Burgess, Bowden, Davey Smith), and where the audience is comfortable with both formal methods and applied neuroepidemiological examples. The paper directly extends the IJE triangulation literature with mathematical formalism, making it a natural successor piece. European Journal of Epidemiology would be a backup.

### Strongest Result
The transport obstruction classification, particularly the smoking-MS and vitamin D-MS cases taken together. These two cases show OPPOSITE discordance patterns (obs+/MR-null vs MR+/RCT-null) and the framework classifies both as EVIDENCE MISFIRE but for different geometric reasons. If the paper can show that the obstruction taxonomy correctly diagnoses the failure mode in each case -- residual confounding for smoking, estimand mismatch (lifetime genetic liability vs. adult supplementation) for vitamin D -- this would be the first formal system that does what triangulation papers currently do informally and inconsistently. The MR community has been arguing about these exact cases for years without a systematic classification.

### Fatal Weakness
The paper treats MR null results as face-value evidence against causation without reporting instrument diagnostics. For smoking-MS, an MR null could reflect weak instruments (low conditional F-statistics), horizontal pleiotropy (violated exclusion restriction), winner's curse in SNP selection, or the fact that MR estimates lifetime genetic liability to smoke rather than actual smoking behavior. The paper classifies this as "confound unmasked" but never demonstrates that the MR study had adequate power to detect the observational effect size, never reports whether MR-Egger, weighted median, or MR-PRESSO sensitivity analyses were concordant with the IVW null, and never acknowledges that the MR estimand (effect of genetic predisposition) differs fundamentally from the observational estimand (effect of behavioral smoking). Any MR reviewer will reject this interpretation immediately. The same problem applies to the IL-6 MR null. Without instrument strength, sensitivity analyses, and estimand specification for every MR result in the evidence base, the framework is building geometric theory on unchecked foundations.

### Must Add
A systematic MR diagnostics table for every MR result in the evidence base: (1) number of SNPs and conditional F-statistics for instrument strength, (2) results from at least three MR methods with different pleiotropy assumptions (IVW, MR-Egger with intercept test, weighted median), (3) MR-PRESSO or Cochran Q for outlier/heterogeneity detection, (4) explicit statement of the MR estimand versus the comparator estimand, and (5) power calculations showing whether the MR study could have detected the observational effect size. Without this table, every "MR null = confound unmasked" claim is ungrounded. The framework should then incorporate instrument strength and sensitivity concordance into its validity tier assignment -- an MR result with F<10 or discordant sensitivity analyses should receive a lower construct validity score than one with strong, concordant instruments.

### Must Cut
The fiber bundle and Grassmannian formalism should be radically de-emphasized or moved entirely to a mathematical appendix. For an epidemiology audience, the geometric language is an accessibility barrier that will cause desk rejection at IJE. The transport obstruction taxonomy, Frechet variance test, and validity tiers can all be presented in standard statistical language (discordance classification, convergence variance ratio, evidence quality strata) without invoking differential geometry. The mathematical formalism adds nothing to the epidemiological content that cannot be communicated more directly, and it will read to reviewers as obscurantism rather than precision. Keep the math in a supplement for the interdisciplinary claim; lead with the epidemiological substance.

### Key References
1. Lawlor DA, Tilling K, Davey Smith G (2016) "Triangulation in aetiological epidemiology" International Journal of Epidemiology 45(6):1866-1886 -- THE foundational paper on combining evidence across study designs including MR; this paper must position itself as formalizing what Lawlor et al. left qualitative. 2. Burgess S, Davies NM, Thompson SG (2016) "Bias due to participant overlap in two-sample Mendelian randomization" Genetic Epidemiology 40(7):597-608 AND Burgess S et al. (2019) "Guidelines for performing Mendelian randomization investigations" Wellcome Open Research 4:186 -- defines the sensitivity analysis standards that every MR result in the evidence base must meet before being interpreted. 3. Sanderson E, Glymour MM, Holmes MV et al. (2022) "Mendelian randomization" Nature Reviews Methods Primers 2:6 -- the comprehensive MR primer that defines when MR nulls are informative versus uninformative, instrument strength requirements, and the distinction between MR estimands and trial estimands.

## Professor 6 — Biostatistician (meta-analysis, random-effects models, I-squared heterogeneity, publication bias)

### Proposed Framing
Frame the paper as proposing and validating a cross-design convergence statistic (the Frechet variance ratio T(C)) that fills a genuine gap in evidence synthesis methodology: I-squared measures heterogeneity WITHIN a study design, but no standard tool quantifies whether independent evidence TYPES (MR, RCT, observational) converge on the same answer. T(C) does this. The neuroepidemiology evidence base becomes the calibration dataset, and the fiber bundle language is relegated to an appendix as the mathematical motivation for why the statistic works. The paper is then a methods-and-calibration paper, structured as: (1) define the problem (no formal convergence test across evidence families), (2) derive T(C) from Frechet variance, (3) calibrate against 59 ratio-scale estimates spanning two diseases, (4) demonstrate the failure taxonomy when convergence breaks.

### Proposed Title
> Cross-Design Evidence Convergence: A Frechet Variance Test for Mechanistic Realism in Neuroepidemiology

### Main Contribution
A formal test statistic T(C) = Var_F / sigma-squared_null that quantifies whether independent evidence streams (MR, RCT, observational) converge beyond chance, filling the gap between within-design I-squared and informal triangulation narratives.

### Target Journal
Research Synthesis Methods (Wiley) -- this is the methodological home for novel meta-analytic test statistics with applied calibration; the cross-design convergence problem is squarely in scope, the journal expects formal derivation plus empirical validation, and the readership (evidence synthesis methodologists) would immediately recognize the I-squared gap this fills. Statistics in Medicine is a backup if the applied neuroepidemiology angle is strengthened.

### Strongest Result
The failed AD RCT cluster: T = 0.004 with p_convergence approximately 1e-6 across 6 independent null drugs. This is the cleanest demonstration because all six are independent phase-III trials testing different molecular targets in different populations, yet they converge to HR approximately 1.0 with extraordinary precision. A biostatistician would find this compelling because it shows the test correctly identifies a known ground truth (the amyloid hypothesis fails therapeutically) with strong statistical significance and no cherry-picking concerns -- the null result IS the convergence.

### Fatal Weakness
Non-independence of observations inflates every reported p-value. The analysis treats all 59 rows as independent, but they are not: MS-033 and MS-033b come from the same FREEDOMS II trial; AD-005n through AD-005o2 share overlapping primary studies across meta-analyses; APOE4 het/hom/women estimates are from the same genetic analysis. The Kendall tau p-values from scipy.stats.kendalltau assume exchangeable independent pairs. With clustering by claim family (roughly 18 groups), the effective sample size drops to perhaps 25-30 independent units. The meta-regression similarly lacks any random-effects or cluster-robust standard errors. A reviewer at Research Synthesis Methods will immediately flag this. Additionally, the null variance sigma-squared_null is computed as the marginal variance across ALL 59 rows mixing diseases, designs, and effect types -- this is not the correct null for a convergence test, which should be the expected within-family variance under independence conditional on the marginal distribution of each family member.

### Must Add
A cluster-robust reanalysis that properly accounts for the hierarchical structure of the data. Specifically: (1) fit a mixed-effects meta-regression with random intercepts for claim family and disease, using the lme4/statsmodels mixed-model API, and report cluster-robust standard errors alongside the OLS results; (2) derive the correct null distribution for T(C) via permutation -- shuffle family membership labels 10,000 times to get an empirical null, rather than relying on the analytic concentration bound which assumes independence; (3) compute I-squared within each Frechet family to show the relationship between T(C) and classical heterogeneity; (4) present a funnel plot or Egger's test for the full dataset to address the selection bias concern. Without these, any biostatistician reviewer will reject on methodological grounds.

### Must Cut
The fiber bundle, Grassmannian, and transport cocycle formalism should be cut from the main text entirely and moved to a mathematical appendix. A biostatistics audience will view these as unnecessary abstraction barriers to understanding what is fundamentally a variance-ratio test with a permutation null. The obstruction classification (Section 5 of the analysis) can be presented as a taxonomy of convergence failures using plain epidemiological language (evidence misfire = design-dependent confounding; mimic mechanism = surrogate endpoint failure; reference debt = effect modification by population). The geometric vocabulary adds no statistical content and will alienate the target readership.

### Key References
(1) Higgins and Thompson 2002, "Quantifying heterogeneity in a meta-analysis" (Statistics in Medicine) -- the I-squared paper. This is the direct ancestor; T(C) must be explicitly positioned as the cross-design analogue of I-squared, showing where I-squared stops (within-design) and T(C) begins (across-design). (2) Lawlor, Tilling, and Davey Smith 2016, "Triangulation in aetiological epidemiology" (International Journal of Epidemiology) -- the most-cited formalization of evidence triangulation; the invariance depth delta is essentially a quantification of their qualitative framework, and this must be acknowledged and compared. (3) IntHout, Ioannidis, and Borm 2014, "The Hartung-Knapp-Sidik-Jonkman method for random effects meta-analysis is straightforward and considerably outperforms the standard DerSimonian-Laird method" (BMC Medical Research Methodology) -- to justify using proper random-effects methods rather than OLS, and to show awareness of the well-known problems with naive variance estimation in small-sample meta-analysis.

## Professor 10: Journal Editor (Epidemiology / Statistics)

### Proposed Framing
Frame this as a quantitative formalization of triangulation -- the epidemiological concept that causal claims require convergent evidence from independent study designs. The paper introduces two operational tools: (1) a variance-ratio convergence test that quantifies WHEN independent evidence streams agree beyond chance, and (2) a six-mode taxonomy of HOW triangulation fails (evidence misfire, mimic mechanism, reference debt). Demonstrate these on 59 neuroepidemiology claims spanning MS and AD, showing that the convergence test discriminates replicable from fragmented evidence and that the failure taxonomy captures patterns (such as the vitamin D MR-RCT discordance and fingolimod relapse-vs-progression split) that existing frameworks like GRADE flag as generic "inconsistency" without diagnostic resolution.

### Proposed Title
> A Convergence Test and Failure Taxonomy for Triangulation: Quantifying When and How Independent Evidence Streams Agree in Neuroepidemiology

### Main Contribution
A variance-ratio test statistic for evidence convergence (T = within-family variance / marginal variance, with T far below 1 licensing realism) paired with a structured six-mode failure taxonomy that diagnoses WHY triangulation fails, validated on 59 ratio-scale claims across multiple sclerosis and Alzheimer's disease.

### Target Journal
International Journal of Epidemiology (IJE), Methods and Measures section. IJE published the foundational triangulation papers (Lawlor et al. 2016, Munafo and Davey Smith 2018) and has an active readership primed for methodological advances in evidence synthesis. The sample size (59 claims, 18 claim groups, 6 claim families for the convergence test) fits IJE's methods-illustration format -- substantial enough to demonstrate the tools without requiring a full systematic review. Research Synthesis Methods is the backup if IJE considers the scope too narrow; Statistics in Medicine is the backup if the test-statistic novelty is the selling point.

### Strongest Result
The transport obstruction failure taxonomy. The six classified failure modes -- especially vitamin D (MR causal OR=2.0 but all four supplementation RCTs null), fingolimod (ARR 0.52 works but CDP 0.83 null in the same trial), and TBI (dementia OR=1.81 but AD-specifically null at 1.02) -- provide diagnostic resolution that GRADE and existing triangulation frameworks lack. These are not obscure edge cases: they represent active clinical controversies (should we supplement vitamin D for MS prevention? does fingolimod slow progression?) where a generic label of "inconsistency" fails to explain the discordance. The taxonomy identifies the structural reason: the causal identity rotates under endpoint transport (mimic mechanism) or collapses under design transport (evidence misfire). No existing evidence-grading framework makes these distinctions operationally.

### Fatal Weakness
The geometric formalism (fiber bundles, Grassmannians, connection forms) does not survive contact with the actual data. In the epidemiological application, the Frechet variance is computed on scalar log-effects -- ordinary variance on the real line, not Frechet means on a Grassmannian manifold. The claim space has no fiber bundle structure that is used in any computation. The "sections," "connections," and "cocycles" are metaphors relabeling concepts the field already has names for (effect modification, generalizability, confounding). An epidemiology reviewer will immediately ask: what computation in this paper requires differential geometry rather than standard statistics? The honest answer is none. The variance-ratio test is a one-sample analogue of I-squared heterogeneity; the failure taxonomy is a refined version of GRADE's inconsistency and indirectness domains. Presenting these in fiber-bundle language will trigger desk rejection at any epidemiology journal -- not because the underlying ideas are wrong, but because the mathematical framing obscures rather than enables the actual analytical work. A second concern: the tier assignments incorporate evidence quality and design features that are correlated with effect magnitude through selection and reporting pathways (larger effects survive adjustment more easily, motivate more RCTs, and thus climb tiers), creating a circularity risk in the tau=0.54 correlation that the jackknife cannot address because it is structural, not driven by outliers.

### Must Add
A formal comparison table: MV framework versus GRADE versus Lawlor triangulation versus Bradford Hill criteria, row by row, showing which failure modes each framework can and cannot diagnose. Without this, the paper will be desk-rejected with the comment "how does this improve on GRADE?" The table should demonstrate that GRADE's inconsistency domain conflates all six failure modes into one label, that Bradford Hill's criteria are not operationalized for quantitative testing, and that Lawlor's triangulation framework is qualitative where this paper's convergence test is quantitative. Include a worked example (vitamin D is ideal) showing how each framework would handle the MR-RCT discordance and what diagnostic resolution each provides. This comparison is the difference between a methodological contribution and a mathematical exercise.

### Must Cut
The fiber bundle and Grassmannian formalism must be removed from the main text entirely and placed in a technical appendix for mathematically inclined readers. Every proposition (3.2 through 3.4) should be restated in plain statistical language in the main text: "convergence test" not "Frechet variance on sections," "failure mode" not "transport obstruction cocycle," "evidence quality tier" not "superlevel-set filtration." The claim that this framework "transfers without modification from AI interpretability" should be replaced with a brief motivation paragraph noting that the operational tools were inspired by geometric methods in machine learning but have been reformulated for epidemiological application. The current framing will alienate the entire readership.

### Key References
(1) Lawlor DA, Tilling K, Davey Smith G. Triangulation in aetiological epidemiology. International Journal of Epidemiology 2016;45(6):1866-1886 -- the foundational paper defining triangulation as convergence of independent study designs toward the same causal conclusion. This paper's convergence test operationalizes exactly what Lawlor left qualitative. (2) Higgins JPT, Thompson SG. Quantifying heterogeneity in a meta-analysis. Statistics in Medicine 2002;21(11):1539-1558 -- introduced the I-squared statistic for between-study heterogeneity. The Frechet variance ratio T is a within-family analogue: where I-squared asks whether studies of the same design disagree, T asks whether studies of different designs agree. The paper must show that T is not simply I-squared repackaged. (3) Guyatt GH et al. GRADE: an emerging consensus on rating quality of evidence and strength of recommendations. BMJ 2008;336:924-926 -- the dominant evidence-grading framework that this paper implicitly competes with. Failure to engage with GRADE will be fatal at peer review; the failure taxonomy must be positioned as a diagnostic refinement of GRADE's inconsistency and indirectness domains.

## Professor 5: Philosopher of Science (Mechanism) -- working on mechanistic explanation in the tradition of Machamer, Darden, Craver, and Bechtel

### Proposed Framing
The paper should be framed as addressing an open problem in the philosophy of mechanistic explanation: What formal structure, if any, do mechanism claims have across scientific domains, and how should we adjudicate when a mechanism claim fails to refer? The new mechanism movement (MDC 2000, Craver 2007) gave us an ontology of entities and activities but left the epistemology of mechanism claims informal -- "how-possibly" vs "how-actually" distinctions, sketches vs schemas, levels of mechanistic detail. This paper proposes that mechanism claims have a specific geometric structure (sections of a fiber bundle over a claim space) and that this structure is domain-invariant, testable via convergence (Frechet variance) and diagnosable via transport failure modes. The neuroepidemiology application is not a metaphor but a genuine test: if the same formal framework calibrates in both AI circuits and clinical epidemiology without modification, this constitutes evidence for a domain-general structure of mechanistic reasoning itself.

### Proposed Title
> The Geometry of Mechanism Claims: Convergence, Transport, and Reference Failure Across Domains

### Main Contribution
A formal framework that gives mechanism claims a testable geometric structure -- fiber bundle sections whose validity is diagnosed by convergence (Frechet variance) and whose reference failures are classified by transport obstructions -- shown to calibrate quantitatively (tau=0.54) across 59 neuroepidemiology claims spanning two diseases, providing evidence that "mechanism" has domain-invariant formal structure beyond the informal ontology of the new mechanism movement.

### Target Journal
Philosophy of Science (journal of the Philosophy of Science Association), because this directly addresses the central question of whether mechanistic explanation has formal structure beyond informal characterizations, engages the MDC/Craver tradition that dominates the journal's readership, and the empirical calibration results distinguish it from purely conceptual contributions. Studies in History and Philosophy of Science is the alternative if the cross-domain transfer angle is emphasized over the formal structure angle. Avoid British Journal for Philosophy of Science -- the heavy mathematical apparatus risks appearing as formalism for its own sake to their reviewers without the empirical payoff being foregrounded.

### Strongest Result
The transport obstruction classification would most impress philosophers of mechanism. The six failure modes (evidence misfire, mimic mechanism, reference debt) formalize distinctions that philosophers have gestured at informally for two decades. The anti-tau CSF case -- 99% pharmacological target engagement with 0% clinical benefit -- is a textbook "mimic mechanism" that makes vivid a category the MDC framework cannot express: an entity-activity organization that is productive of regular changes at one level of description but fails to constitute a mechanism at the clinically relevant level. The APOE4 reference debt case (OR varying 1.9 to 4.5 across ancestries with no global section) formalizes a failure mode that connects directly to debates about natural kinds and reference stability in philosophy of science. These are not ad hoc categories but fall out of the geometric framework as distinct topological obstructions.

### Fatal Weakness
The framework conflates two things philosophers carefully distinguish: the epistemology of mechanism claims (how we know about mechanisms) and the ontology of mechanisms themselves (what mechanisms are). The fiber bundle formalism operates entirely at the epistemic level -- it tracks convergence of evidence, validity of claims, and reference stability of descriptions. But the paper's rhetoric implies ontological conclusions: that mechanisms "are" geometric objects, that "mechanism" is a geometric concept. Craver's mutual manipulability account, Bechtel's decomposition-and-localization strategy, and the MDC entities-and-activities ontology are claims about what mechanisms are in the world, not about how we assess claims about them. A sophisticated validity assessment framework -- even one with elegant mathematics -- does not establish that mechanisms have geometric structure. It establishes that our epistemic access to mechanisms can be usefully modeled geometrically. A reviewer from the Craver school will drive a truck through this gap. The paper must either (a) explicitly restrict itself to the epistemic level and argue that is sufficient and interesting, or (b) mount a genuine argument from structural realism that the epistemic structure reflects ontic structure, which requires engaging Ladyman, French, and the structural realism literature directly.

### Must Add
A section explicitly engaging the Russo-Williamson thesis -- that causal claims in medicine require both difference-making evidence (statistical, epidemiological) and mechanistic evidence. This is THE central philosophical claim about mechanisms in medicine (Russo and Williamson 2007, "Interpreting Causality in the Health Sciences," International Studies in the Philosophy of Science). The MV framework's split calibration result -- where etiologic and therapeutic strata have different slopes -- directly speaks to this thesis by showing that difference-making evidence (effect magnitudes) and mechanistic evidence (validity tiers) interact differently depending on whether the claim is about etiology or intervention. Without engaging Russo-Williamson, the paper will appear to philosophers of medicine as reinventing a wheel while ignoring the existing conversation. Additionally, the paper needs to address the relationship between the fiber bundle formalism and simpler ordinal scoring: a philosopher will ask whether Kendall tau=0.54 with a 5-level ordinal scale genuinely requires Grassmannians and connections, or whether the geometric apparatus is doing no work that a simple quality ladder could not do. The paper must identify at least one result that the geometry delivers and that ordinal scoring cannot.

### Must Cut
The Grassmannian fiber Gr(k,d) as the identity space for mechanism claims. In the AI interpretability setting where you are literally working with subspaces of activation space, the Grassmannian is the natural mathematical object. In neuroepidemiology, there is no d-dimensional vector space whose k-dimensional subspaces correspond to mechanism identities. The claim that hazard ratios and odds ratios live in a Grassmannian fiber is either false or so metaphorical as to be misleading. This mismatch undermines the central "domain-invariant structure" claim. Either restrict the Grassmannian to the AI domain and use a simpler fiber (a real-valued effect space, or a discrete set of mechanistic pathways) for epidemiology, or explain precisely what the Grassmannian parameterizes in the clinical setting. Forcing the same fiber type across domains when it fits one and not the other is exactly the kind of Procrustean formalism that makes philosophers suspicious of mathematical frameworks in non-mathematical domains.

## Professor 1: Causal Inference Methodologist (Pearl/Bareinboim school)

### Proposed Framing
Frame this as a formal transportability analysis: the MV framework defines a category of experimental contexts (observational, MR, RCT) with transport operators between them, and the neuroepidemiology data provides 59 ratio-scale test cases where transport either succeeds (tier preserved, estimate stable) or fails (tier drops, estimate diverges). The paper's core contribution is showing that the five failure modes classified by the framework (evidence misfire, mimic mechanism, reference debt, etc.) correspond to identifiable violations of Pearl/Bareinboim transportability conditions --- specifically, that "transport obstructions" are empirically detectable via the Frechet variance test statistic T(C). This reframes the MV framework as providing a practical taxonomy of transportability failures, calibrated against real epidemiological data where ground truth (MR vs RCT agreement/disagreement) is available.

### Proposed Title
> Transport Obstructions in Mechanism Claims: A Geometric Validity Framework Calibrated on 59 Neuroepidemiology Effect Estimates

### Main Contribution
The Frechet variance realism test (T statistic) provides a computable, single-number diagnostic for whether independent evidence streams converge beyond chance, operationalizing the concept of transportability failure across experimental designs (MR, RCT, observational) with calibrated thresholds validated on 59 neuroepidemiology claims spanning MS and AD.

### Target Journal
International Journal of Epidemiology (IJE) --- Methods and Statistics section. Rationale: IJE publishes methodological papers that bridge causal inference theory with epidemiological practice, has a strong tradition of MR methodology papers, and its readership includes both the epi-methods community that would scrutinize the transportability claims and the neuroepidemiology community that would recognize the clinical examples. The paper's 59-row evidence base with verified CIs, MR negative controls, and RCT data meets IJE's empirical standards. Alternatively, Epidemiology (Lippincott) if the methodological emphasis is too heavy for IJE, or Statistics in Medicine if the formal framework needs more technical exposition than IJE allows.

### Strongest Result
The six transport obstruction case studies, especially vitamin D (MR causal OR=2.0 but RCTs null) and smoking (observational positive, MR null OR=1.03). These are textbook examples where the identity of the causal effect does not transport across experimental designs, and the framework classifies the failure mode correctly. The fact that the Frechet variance test assigns T=0.004 to the failed AD RCTs (six independent null drugs converging to HR approximately 1.0) is striking --- it detects a real pattern (amyloid-targeting drugs consistently fail) that has enormous clinical significance and licenses the "realism" verdict for what is essentially a strong null result. This would resonate deeply with epidemiologists who have watched the amyloid hypothesis collapse drug by drug.

### Fatal Weakness
The Frechet variance test (Proposition 3.3) is presented as a causal identification strategy but is formally a descriptive convergence diagnostic. The test statistic T(C) = Var_F(C) / sigma_null^2 measures whether effect estimates cluster more tightly than marginal variance would predict. This is a test of homogeneity, not a test of causation. Low T can arise from (a) genuine causal convergence, (b) correlated biases across studies (shared confounders, publication bias, shared data sources), or (c) mechanistically unrelated estimates that happen to be similar in magnitude. The paper must explicitly address the identifiability gap: Frechet convergence is NECESSARY for realism but not SUFFICIENT, and the causal interpretation requires additional assumptions (independence of evidence streams, no shared confounders across study designs) that must be stated and defended. The connection to do-calculus is gestural rather than formal --- the transport obstruction classification uses cocycle language but never demonstrates that these obstructions correspond to failures of specific do-calculus transport formulas. A Pearl/Bareinboim reviewer would ask: what is the selection diagram? Where are the S-nodes? What exactly is being transported, and under what conditions does the transport formula hold?

### Must Add
A formal mapping between the five transport obstruction failure modes and Pearl/Bareinboim selection diagrams. For each failure mode (evidence misfire, mimic mechanism, reference debt, etc.), draw the selection diagram with S-nodes indicating which variables change across contexts, state the transportability formula, and show exactly where it fails. For example, "evidence misfire" (vitamin D: MR causal, RCT null) should be mapped to a selection diagram where the MR context and the RCT context differ by an S-node on a mediator or modifier, making the do-calculus transport formula inapplicable. Without this, the framework's vocabulary is evocative but not formally grounded in the transportability theory it invokes. Additionally, the paper needs a sensitivity analysis for the independence assumption underlying the Frechet test --- when two studies share GWAS data or overlapping cohorts, the convergence is not independent and T(C) is biased downward.

### Must Cut
The fiber bundle formalism (sections, connections, Grassmannian identity spaces) should be substantially de-emphasized or moved to an appendix. For an epidemiology audience, the fiber bundle language adds cognitive load without carrying identification content --- the propositions can be stated in terms of heterogeneity testing, meta-analytic convergence, and transportability conditions without invoking differential geometry. The core empirical results (Kendall tau, meta-regression, Frechet variance, obstruction classification) are accessible and valuable on their own. Foregrounding the fiber bundle formalism risks triggering exactly the reviewer response that the boundary conditions paper correctly warns against: "this is standard statistics in topological language." Lead with the epidemiological content and the transportability connection; put the geometric foundations in an appendix for readers coming from the AI interpretability context.

### Key References
1. Pearl and Bareinboim (2011, AAAI) "Transportability of Causal and Statistical Relations: A Formal Approach" --- the foundational paper on transportability with selection diagrams. The transport obstruction classification MUST be mapped to this formalism or the causal inference community will view it as reinventing the wheel in unfamiliar notation. 2. VanderWeele and Ding (2017, Annals of Internal Medicine) "Sensitivity Analysis in Observational Research: Introducing the E-Value" --- the E-value framework provides the standard approach to assessing robustness of causal claims to unmeasured confounding in epidemiology; the Frechet variance test needs to be compared to and positioned against E-values as complementary diagnostics. 3. Higgins and Thompson (2002, Statistics in Medicine) "Quantifying heterogeneity in a meta-analysis" --- the I-squared statistic is the standard tool for assessing between-study heterogeneity, and the Frechet variance test must be explicitly compared to I-squared to clarify what it adds (the claim should be that T(C) tests convergence ACROSS evidence families, not just within a homogeneous study design, which I-squared does not address).

## Meta-Epidemiologist / GRADE Expert

### Proposed Framing
Frame this as an empirical comparison between a formal geometric validity framework and existing evidence-grading systems (GRADE, Bradford Hill), using a neuroepidemiology evidence base as a test bed. The paper should lead with the transport obstruction taxonomy and the split calibration finding as actionable contributions that expose specific gaps in current meta-analytic practice, then present the fiber bundle mathematics as the generating formalism rather than the headline. The argument is: GRADE handles inconsistency and indirectness as single downgrade domains, but this framework decomposes them into mechanistically distinct failure modes (evidence misfire vs. mimic mechanism vs. reference debt) that require different corrective actions -- and it empirically demonstrates that etiologic and therapeutic evidence streams require separate calibration scales, something GRADE implicitly assumes but never formalizes or validates.

### Proposed Title
> Beyond Inconsistency and Indirectness: A Geometric Framework for Mechanistic Failure Modes in Evidence Synthesis, with Calibration Evidence from Neuroepidemiology

### Main Contribution
The transport obstruction taxonomy decomposes GRADE's coarse "inconsistency" and "indirectness" domains into six mechanistically distinct failure modes -- evidence misfire, claim laundering, mimic mechanism, reference debt, zombie mechanism, and domain shift -- each requiring different corrective actions in systematic reviews, and the split calibration finding (tau_etiologic = 0.37 vs tau_therapeutic = 0.62, interaction p = 0.032) demonstrates that etiologic and therapeutic evidence occupy incommensurable effect-magnitude regimes that a single grading scale cannot calibrate.

### Target Journal
International Journal of Epidemiology -- it publishes methodological innovations that challenge established frameworks (GRADE critiques, causal inference methods, Bradford Hill reappraisals), has a readership comfortable with formal methods beyond clinical heuristics, and the cross-disciplinary origin story (AI interpretability to epidemiology) fits its tradition of importing mathematical tools from adjacent fields. Journal of Clinical Epidemiology is tempting as the GRADE home journal, but the fiber bundle formalism would face reflexive rejection from GRADE loyalists who see the framework as settled; IJE's editorial stance is more receptive to provocative methodological contributions.

### Strongest Result
The mimic mechanism failure mode, exemplified by anti-tau CSF biomarkers (99% pharmacological target engagement, 0% clinical benefit in the same patients). GRADE would rate this as "high certainty of no effect," which is technically correct but mechanistically vacuous -- it tells the reviewer nothing about WHY the mechanism failed or what it implies for the next trial targeting the same pathway. The MV framework identifies this as a specific obstruction type (the mechanism section exists in the surrogate fiber but does not transport to the clinical outcome fiber), which is a genuinely new diagnostic category that no existing evidence-grading system captures. The vitamin D case (MR says causal at OR=2.0, five RCTs consistently null) as evidence misfire rather than inconsistency is equally compelling -- GRADE would downgrade for inconsistency and leave it at that, while the framework identifies a specific causal structure (confounding unmasked by instrumental variable) that explains the discrepancy.

### Fatal Weakness
The central quantitative finding conflates validity tier with effect magnitude, and this conflation is precisely what GRADE explicitly warns against. GRADE separates certainty of evidence (how sure we are) from magnitude of effect (how big it is). High-certainty evidence can show small effects; low-certainty evidence can show large effects. By regressing |log(effect)| on tier, the paper is claiming that higher-validity claims have larger effects -- but this likely reflects a circularity: associations with large, easily detectable effects (EBV with HR=32.4) naturally accumulate more evidence from more independent study designs and thus get assigned higher tiers, while small effects (therapeutic HRs near 1.0) are intrinsically harder to replicate and thus accumulate less evidence. The R-squared of 0.38 (even 0.68 with WLS) could be entirely driven by this evidence-accumulation artifact rather than by the framework capturing genuine validity structure. A GRADE reviewer will immediately ask: "If I gave you 20 claims all with HR=1.05 but from massive, well-designed RCTs, would your framework assign them low tiers because the effects are small?" If yes, the framework is confusing precision with magnitude. If no, the correlation is an artifact of the evidence base composition. Either way, the paper needs a direct test using a validity-relevant outcome (replication success, subsequent retraction, clinical guideline uptake) rather than effect size as the dependent variable.

### Must Add
A head-to-head comparison table where every row in the 59-claim evidence base receives both a GRADE rating (Very Low / Low / Moderate / High) and an MV tier (1-5), with formal agreement statistics (weighted kappa, Bland-Altman plot of tier assignments) and -- critically -- identification of the specific claims where the two systems disagree and why. Without this, the paper cannot answer the most basic question any systematic reviewer will ask: "What does this framework tell me that GRADE does not?" The six transport obstruction cases are anecdotal evidence of divergence; a systematic comparison across all 59 claims would demonstrate whether the MV framework adds signal or merely repackages existing GRADE judgments in geometric language. Additionally, include at least one case where GRADE outperforms MV (e.g., GRADE's handling of imprecision and publication bias, which MV appears to lack entirely) to show intellectual honesty about the framework's limitations.

### Must Cut
The fiber bundle formalism (sections of bundles, Grassmannian fibers, connection transport, cocycle obstruction) should be moved entirely to an online supplement or appendix. For the main text, translate every geometric concept into epidemiological language: "transport obstruction" becomes "failure of evidence to generalize across study designs," "Frechet variance" becomes "convergence statistic across independent evidence streams," "section of a bundle" becomes "consistent mechanism interpretation across contexts." The mathematics may be correct, but in its current form it creates a 50-page barrier to entry for the 99% of systematic reviewers who lack differential geometry training. If the framework's value depends on readers understanding fiber bundles, it will never be adopted; if it does not depend on that understanding, then the formalism is notational overhead that obscures the operational contributions. The Grassmannian identity space is particularly gratuitous in the epidemiology application -- mechanism identity in clinical evidence is not naturally a subspace of a d-dimensional vector space, and forcing it into that mold imports precision without accuracy.

1) Balshem et al. (2011) "GRADE guidelines: 3. Rating the quality of evidence" (Journal of Clinical Epidemiology) -- the foundational paper on GRADE's domain structure that the transport obstruction taxonomy must explicitly extend or correct; without engaging this paper the framework appears unaware of the existing evidence-grading architecture it claims to improve upon. 2) Fedak et al.
